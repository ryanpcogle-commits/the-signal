Lead Story

Anthropic Accidentally Ships Its Own Source Code — Then Scrambles to Erase It

An accidental release of 512,000 lines of Claude Code internals exposed codenamed references to Anthropic's next frontier model — and sparked the most chaotic 24 hours in the company's history.

On April 1, 2026, a routine Claude Code release included approximately 1,900 internal files and 512,000 lines of source code that were never meant to be public. Anthropic's statement was terse: "Earlier today, a Claude Code release included some internal source code. No sensitive customer data or credentials were involved or exposed." Within hours, thousands of GitHub forks had appeared and engineers across the industry were combing through the repository.

What they found went beyond plumbing. Internal documents embedded in the release included references to a next-generation model codenamed Capybara — also referenced in a separate earlier leak as Claude Mythos. Internal descriptions called it "by far the most powerful AI model we have ever developed," sitting in a new tier above Opus. According to leaked text, training is complete and early access testing with cybersecurity partners is already underway.

Anthropic issued copyright takedown requests to remove the copies — later acknowledging the takedowns were broader than intended, catching unrelated repositories in the sweep. Prediction markets now give roughly a 25% chance of a public Mythos announcement before April 30. The incident underscores how thin the margin has become between internal development and public exposure at the speed today's AI labs operate.

Gemini 3.1 Pro Tops 13 of 16 Benchmarks — Including a Record 77.1% on ARC-AGI-2

Google DeepMind's Gemini 3.1 Pro is now the benchmark leader across most major evaluations, scoring 77.1% on ARC-AGI-2 — more than double the score of Gemini 3 Pro — and 94.3% on GPQA Diamond. It matches GPT-5.4 Pro on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index at roughly one-third of the API cost. A companion model, Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, delivers 2.5x faster response times and 45% faster output generation at just $0.25 per million input tokens, making frontier-caliber speed genuinely affordable for the first time.

OpenAI Closes $122B Round at $852B Valuation — IPO Steps Begin

OpenAI closed the largest startup funding round in history, raising $122 billion with Amazon contributing $50 billion, NVIDIA $30 billion, and SoftBank $30 billion (notably, $35 billion of Amazon's commitment is contingent on OpenAI reaching an IPO or AGI milestone). Robotics

Boston Dynamics Integrates Gemini Robotics Into Atlas — No Cloud Required

Boston Dynamics and Google DeepMind announced a formal partnership to integrate Gemini Robotics — a vision-language-action model suite — directly into Boston Dynamics' Atlas humanoid robot. The models run on-device without cloud connectivity and can generalize to new situations from limited examples. The collaboration marks a significant maturation of physical AI: instead of scripted motions, Atlas can now reason about novel environments and adapt in real time using the same underlying model family powering consumer AI products.

Models

Grok 4.20 Introduces Four-Agent Architecture That Debates Before Answering

xAI's Grok 4.20 takes a genuinely novel approach: instead of a single larger model, every complex query is handled by four specialized agents running in parallel — Grok (coordination), Harper (fact-checking with live X data), Benjamin (logic and coding), and Lucas (creative reasoning) — which debate in real time before producing a single unified output. API access remains pending as of early April. Meanwhile, Grok 5, reportedly built on a 6-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts architecture, is in training and expected in Q2 — which would make it the largest model ever publicly announced.

AI Is Now Predicting Research Trends Two Years Out — And Diagnosing Rare Diseases Faster

Two research breakthroughs highlight AI moving from productivity tool to scientific collaborator. A study published in Nature Machine Intelligence by Karlsruhe Institute of Technology shows that AI combining LLMs and machine learning can analyze scientific literature and predict emerging research trends two to three years in advance — tested and validated on materials science publications. Separately, a new algorithm called EvORanker compares genetic evolutionary patterns across 1,000+ species to detect hidden gene-disease relationships, dramatically accelerating rare disease diagnoses that previously took years. Researchers are already applying it to Stage 4 cancer regression cases.

5 Patterns Worth Watching

  1. The leak economy is accelerating. Anthropic's accidental source code release is the latest in a pattern — Claude Mythos details, internal pricing memos, and training data details are all surfacing faster than legal teams can suppress them. Closed AI development is increasingly porous.
  2. ARC-AGI-2 is the new benchmark that matters. Gemini 3.1 Pro's 77.1% score on ARC-AGI-2 — a test of genuine novel problem-solving that can't be memorized — marks the first time a model has meaningfully closed the gap on a benchmark designed to resist frontier AI. Watch this number closely.
  3. Physical AI is graduating from demos to deployments. Boston Dynamics + Gemini Robotics isn't a research partnership — it's a production integration. On-device, no-cloud reasoning is now a real capability in commercial hardware. The era of AI leaving the screen is arriving faster than most expected.
  4. VC concentration is reaching dangerous levels. Four companies capturing 64% of $297 billion in one quarter means the rest of the startup ecosystem is competing for scraps. The winner-take-most dynamic in AI infrastructure is already visible in funding data before products ship.
  5. Open-source is now a viable frontier-tier option. DeepSeek V3.2 at $0.28 per million tokens versus $2.50 for GPT-5.4. Meta's Llama 4 Maverick at 400B parameters running free on self-hosted hardware. The economics argument for closed APIs is weakening for any team that can manage self-hosting.
