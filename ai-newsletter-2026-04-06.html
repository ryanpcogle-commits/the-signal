Lead Story

OpenAI Hits $852 Billion Valuation as GPT-5.5 Prepares to Drop

A $122 billion funding round, an IPO on the horizon, and a next-generation model already through pretraining — OpenAI is playing a different game than everyone else.

OpenAI closed the largest funding round in technology history this week — $122 billion at an $852 billion valuation, anchored by Amazon ($50B), Nvidia ($30B), and SoftBank ($30B), with an additional $3 billion raised from retail investors. Annualized revenue has surpassed $25 billion, with enterprise now representing 40% of total revenue and growing toward parity with consumer by end of year.

Simultaneously, the company confirmed that GPT-5.5 — internally codenamed "Spud" — has completed pretraining and is expected to ship in April. GPT-5.4, released in early March, already set records on computer-use benchmarks: 75.0% on OSWorld-Verified (surpassing the human baseline of 72.4% for the first time) and 83% on OpenAI's GDPval knowledge-work test. GPT-4o was fully retired from all plans effective April 3rd.

IPO preparations are underway. A public listing could come as soon as late 2026, which would make OpenAI potentially the most valuable tech IPO of the decade — a remarkable trajectory for a company that started as a nonprofit research lab less than a decade ago.

This Week in AI
Models

Google's Gemma 4 Is the Open-Source Model That Changes the Comparison

Google released Gemma 4 on April 2nd — its most capable open model series to date, distilled directly from Gemini 3. The lineup spans four sizes: a 31B dense model (currently ranked #3 on the Arena AI text leaderboard), a 26B Mixture-of-Experts model, and two smaller edge variants. The 26B MoE achieves roughly 97% of the dense 31B quality while activating far fewer parameters per token. Context windows reach 256K tokens with native vision, audio, and 140+ languages. Licensed under Apache 2.0 — more permissive than Meta's Llama 4 — and positioned explicitly as Google's answer to Chinese open-weight models. On-device via Android AICore Developer Preview drops the same week.

Anthropic's "Mythos 5" Leak: A 10-Trillion-Parameter Step Change

Fortune confirmed that Anthropic is internally testing a model codenamed "Mythos 5" — a 10-trillion-parameter system engineered for high-stakes environments including cybersecurity, academic research, and complex coding. The model was internally described as "by far the most powerful AI model we have ever developed" and represents a new tier above the existing Opus line. Originally leaked on March 26th, Anthropic is now providing early access to select cybersecurity partners. An April or Q2 release to the broader public is expected. Anthropic's annualized revenue is approaching $19 billion ahead of the launch.

Models

Microsoft Breaks Cover With Three In-House Foundation Models

Microsoft launched three new MAI (Microsoft AI) foundation models available immediately through Microsoft Foundry: MAI-Transcribe-1, delivering state-of-the-art speech-to-text across the top 25 languages at 2.5x the speed of Azure's previous Fast offering; MAI-Voice-1, a voice generation engine; and MAI-Image-2, an upgraded image creator. VentureBeat called the move "a direct competitive shot at OpenAI and Google." It marks the clearest signal yet that Microsoft, long seen as OpenAI's primary distribution channel, is building AI capabilities of its own.

Infrastructure

DeepSeek V4: Frontier Performance, $5.2 Million Training Cost

DeepSeek's latest model, V4, is a 1-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts system released under Apache 2.0 open weights — and it's performing at a level competitive with US frontier models including Claude Opus 4.6. The estimated training cost was $5.2 million, a fraction of what Western labs spend on comparable systems. DeepSeek V3.2 is already available at roughly $0.28 per million input tokens versus $2+ for Western flagship models. The efficiency gap is forcing every major lab to rethink cost assumptions across the entire stack.

Infrastructure

AlphaEvolve Rewrites Its Own Game Theory Algorithms — and Wins

Google DeepMind's AlphaEvolve — a Gemini-powered system that autonomously mutates code to design better algorithms — was applied to multi-agent reinforcement learning this week. The result: a new variant called VAD-CFR that matched or surpassed state-of-the-art performance in 10 out of 11 complex game environments. Separately, AlphaEvolve has already raised the lower bound on the kissing number in 11 dimensions from 592 to 593 — a pure mathematics result. The system represents a shift from AI that answers questions to AI that designs the tools used to answer questions.

Hardware

Researchers Cut AI Energy Use by 100x — Without Sacrificing Accuracy

A research team published findings on April 5th demonstrating an approach that reduces AI energy consumption by up to 100x while simultaneously improving accuracy. The method combines traditional neural networks with symbolic reasoning — allowing models to offload structured computations to far more efficient symbolic systems rather than approximating everything through gradient descent. If the results hold at scale, the implications for inference cost and data-center power draw are significant. Separately, Google unveiled TurboQuant, a KV-cache compression algorithm that reduces memory requirements by 6x while maintaining frontier model performance.

Policy

Georgia Sends Three AI Bills to the Governor — States Keep Legislating

Georgia's legislature adjourned this week with three AI bills on Governor Brian Kemp's desk: SB 540, requiring chatbot disclosure and child safety protections; SR 789, establishing an AI study committee; and SB 444, prohibiting insurance coverage decisions made solely by AI. The activity mirrors a national trend of states moving faster than Congress on enforceable AI rules. The White House's National Policy Framework, released March 20th, emphasizes innovation and federal preemption over state law — a direct tension with what state legislatures are actually doing. A Quinnipiac poll found more than half of U.S. adults believe AI is likely to cause harm through job displacement and privacy violations.

5 Patterns Worth Watching

  1. OpenAI is on an IPO trajectory. $852B valuation, $25B annualized revenue, and early listing preparations. The company is moving from AI lab to public corporation faster than most expected. Governance questions that were theoretical are now imminent.
  2. Open-source is matching frontier — for a fraction of the cost. DeepSeek V4 at $5.2M training cost. Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0. The economic case for closed frontier models is getting harder to make for every use case below the very top tier.
  3. The model race is now a three-way tie at the top. AI researchers are calling this "the most consequential month in AI model history." Anthropic leads on coding and agentic tasks, Google is the most improved, and OpenAI retains distribution advantages. The gap between them is measured in percentage points.
  4. AI is designing AI. AlphaEvolve evolving game theory algorithms that outperform human-designed ones is not a benchmark result — it's a capability shift. The line between "AI as tool" and "AI as researcher" is blurring faster than the policy conversation can track.
  5. Microsoft is not just an OpenAI reseller anymore. Three proprietary foundation models in a single week signals a fundamental change in Microsoft's AI strategy. Its distribution relationship with OpenAI may now be a competitive liability as much as an asset.
