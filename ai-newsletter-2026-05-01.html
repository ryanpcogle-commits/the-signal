Lead Story

Anthropic Is Worth More Than OpenAI — and the Gap Is Widening

With a $900B valuation round imminent and $30B in annualized revenue, Anthropic has quietly overtaken its better-known rival in both market value and commercial momentum.

Anthropic is in advanced talks to close a funding round that would value the company at over $900 billion — surpassing OpenAI's $852 billion post-money valuation and making it the highest-valued private AI company in the world. The round, which sources say could close within two weeks, follows Google's announcement last week of a commitment to invest up to $40 billion in Anthropic, with $10 billion arriving immediately and the remainder tied to performance milestones.

The commercial picture backs the valuation. Anthropic topped the global LLM revenue table in Q1 2026 with a 31.4% market share, narrowly ahead of OpenAI's 29%. The company has reached $30 billion in annualized revenue — a figure that analysts note reflects a "fewer users, fatter wallets" strategy: enterprise and government contracts at premium price points rather than mass-market consumer subscriptions.

The irony is not lost on the industry. A significant share of Google's and Amazon's recently reported "blowout AI profits" came from their Anthropic stakes, not their core cloud businesses. As Fortune noted, investors are effectively buying exposure to Anthropic through the hyperscalers — and that dynamic has only strengthened the case for Anthropic to raise at a historically unprecedented valuation.

GPT-5.5 Begins Rolling Out — OpenAI Calls It a Step Toward "Solving Cybersecurity"

OpenAI has started rolling out GPT-5.5 to Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise users in ChatGPT and Codex, with GPT-5.5 Pro available to Pro, Business, and Enterprise subscribers. The company describes the release as "an incremental but important step" toward AI capable of solving intractable real-world problems — specifically citing cybersecurity as the near-term target domain. The rollout is staged, with availability expanding over the coming days. No separate API release date has been announced.

Models

Google Ships Gemini 3.1 Ultra and Open-Source Gemma 4 in the Same Week

Google's most significant model release of the year arrived with Gemini 3.1 Ultra, featuring a 2-million token context window that operates natively across text, image, audio, and video without transcription intermediaries. Separately, Google released Gemma 4 — its latest open-source series built for advanced reasoning and agentic workflows — under the Apache 2.0 license, making it freely available for commercial use. The dual release signals Google's intent to compete simultaneously at the frontier and in the open-source ecosystem.

Hardware

NVIDIA Unveils Ising: Open-Source AI Models Designed to Fix Quantum Computing

NVIDIA unveiled Ising, the world's first family of open-source AI models purpose-built to accelerate quantum computing. The models target the two biggest barriers holding quantum hardware back: error correction and processor calibration — both problems that have historically required custom, hand-tuned solutions for every new chip generation. NVIDIA is positioning Ising as the bridge between classical deep learning and the emerging quantum computing ecosystem, releasing it openly to accelerate adoption across the research community.

Policy

Pentagon Signs AI Deals With Seven Companies — Anthropic Notably Absent

The Department of Defense announced agreements with OpenAI, Google, NVIDIA, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, SpaceX, and Reflection to deploy advanced AI capabilities on the Pentagon's classified Impact Level 6 and 7 networks. Anthropic is conspicuously absent — the company remains in dispute with the DOD over guardrails governing military use of its models, including restrictions on autonomous weapons targeting and mass surveillance. The agreements formalize what has been building for months: a division in the AI industry between companies willing to serve defense applications without restrictions and those that are not.

Business

Microsoft and OpenAI Dissolve Exclusivity — OpenAI Now Free to Run on AWS and Google Cloud

In a significant restructuring of their landmark partnership, Microsoft has ended its Azure revenue-share arrangement with OpenAI while retaining a non-exclusive IP license through 2032. In exchange, OpenAI is now free to serve its products from Amazon Web Services and Google Cloud — ending the exclusivity arrangement that has defined the relationship since 2019. The deal effectively turns OpenAI into a multi-cloud company and intensifies competition among cloud providers for AI workloads. AWS CEO Matt Garman highlighted the partnership at the What's Next with AWS event, announcing expanded agentic AI integrations across Amazon Connect and Quick.

Infrastructure

Researchers Claim 100× Energy Reduction in AI — Without Sacrificing Accuracy

A team of researchers published findings showing a hybrid approach combining neural networks with symbolic reasoning can slash AI energy consumption by up to 100× while actually improving accuracy on complex tasks. The technique teaches models to reason more logically — like humans do — rather than relying on brute-force pattern matching across billions of parameters. The work has immediate implications for robotics, where on-device inference is power-constrained, and for data-center operators facing mounting pressure from regulators and utilities over AI's surging electricity demands.

Business

Novo Nordisk Bets Its Entire Business on OpenAI — From Drug Discovery to Supply Chain

Novo Nordisk, the Danish pharmaceutical giant behind Ozempic and Wegovy, announced a sweeping strategic partnership with OpenAI to integrate AI across every function of the company — drug discovery, clinical trials, manufacturing, supply chains, and commercial operations — with full deployment targeted by end of 2026. The scope of the deal is unusual: most pharma-AI partnerships focus on a single R&D use case. Novo Nordisk is treating AI as an operating system for the entire company, not a tool for one department.

Policy

Google Researchers Warn: AI Agents Are Being Hijacked by Hidden Webpage Instructions

Google's security research team published findings that random web pages are embedding hidden text instructions — invisible to human visitors — designed to manipulate AI agents that browse the web on users' behalf. The instructions use prompt injection to redirect agents toward malicious actions: exfiltrating contact directories, forwarding emails, or performing unauthorized transactions. With agentic AI now routinely given access to email, calendars, and files, the attack surface has expanded dramatically. Google stopped short of offering a complete mitigation, calling it "an industry-wide problem requiring an industry-wide solution."

5 Patterns Worth Watching

  1. Anthropic has quietly won the enterprise AI race. A $900B valuation, $30B in ARR, and a No. 1 revenue rank tell a consistent story: enterprises and governments are paying premium rates for safety-focused AI, and that strategy is now more valuable than OpenAI's consumer-first approach.
  2. The Pentagon is forcing AI companies to pick a side. Seven of the biggest AI players have signed on to classified DoD networks. Anthropic has not. This is no longer a theoretical ethical question — it's a business decision with real revenue consequences on both sides.
  3. OpenAI's Azure exclusivity ending reshapes cloud competition. OpenAI running on AWS and Google Cloud means every cloud provider now has a credible AI story. Microsoft retains the IP license but loses the moat. The hyperscaler race just got more crowded.
  4. Prompt injection is the sleeper security crisis of agentic AI. As AI agents gain access to email, files, and transactions, hidden instructions embedded in web pages become a systemic threat. No current architecture fully prevents it — and awareness is still low outside security circles.
  5. Energy efficiency is becoming a strategic differentiator. A 100× reduction in inference energy would change the economics of AI deployment at every scale — from pocket devices to data centers. The lab that cracks it first gains an enormous cost advantage over rivals burning megawatts per query.
